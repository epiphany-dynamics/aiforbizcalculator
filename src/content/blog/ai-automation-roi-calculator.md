An AI automation ROI calculator turns a fuzzy promise about saving time into a number you can challenge. It estimates the cost of work being done manually, the savings an automation may produce, and how quickly those savings could outweigh the cost.

That makes it useful for screening ideas. It does not make the estimate certain.

The [AI automation calculator](/calculator) on this site starts with a guided questionnaire about your industry, business size, workload, and current processes. It uses those answers with industry assumptions to estimate inefficiency costs and recommend possible automations. This guide explains how to read that report, then shows the fuller calculation you should run when you have an actual implementation quote.

## What the calculator is estimating

The site calculator does not ask you to enter a project price or your exact payroll records. It asks about the way your business operates: team size, revenue range, hours lost, follow-up habits, booking method, current tools, and other workflow details that apply to your industry.

From those answers, it estimates figures such as weekly hours lost, annual inefficiency cost, possible recoverable value, and the share of your problems that automation may address. The recommendation cards also show estimated monthly savings, a monthly cost range, and a rough payback period.

Those numbers are a first pass. They are based partly on industry assumptions, so they should help you decide what to investigate, not what to buy. A report can point you toward appointment reminders or lead follow-up automation. Only a real quote can tell you what your version will cost to build, connect, test, and maintain.

The calculator itself says the figures are informational estimates. Keep that warning attached to every number.

## How to read the recommendation cards

Each recommended automation has a monthly cost range and an estimated monthly savings figure. The calculator uses the midpoint of the cost range as its working monthly cost.

For example, a range of 100 to 300 dollars per month has a midpoint of 200 dollars. If the estimated monthly savings are 800 dollars, the estimated net monthly savings are:

800 dollars minus 200 dollars = 600 dollars per month.

The displayed payback figure is a quick screening estimate built from those monthly values. It does not include a one-time implementation quote because the questionnaire never asks for one. That distinction matters. A card that looks attractive may still fail a full budget check if integration, data cleanup, training, or testing is expensive.

Use the report to shortlist an idea. Then replace its assumptions with your own labor cost, expected time savings, ongoing expense, and implementation quote.

## The five numbers for a full budget check

Once you have a specific project in mind, gather five inputs:

1. Hours the automation will actually save each week
2. The loaded hourly cost of the people doing that work
3. The one-time implementation cost
4. The monthly software and maintenance cost
5. A conservative adjustment for time savings that never materialize

Loaded hourly cost is more than wage. It can include payroll tax, benefits, and other employment overhead. If you do not have a reliable loaded rate, ask whoever handles payroll or bookkeeping. Guessing high or low can distort the result.

Be equally careful with hours saved. If a task takes ten hours now but someone will still spend two hours reviewing exceptions, the saving is eight hours, not ten.

## The formulas in plain arithmetic

Annual labor savings = hours saved per week x 52 x loaded hourly cost

Ongoing annual cost = monthly running cost x 12

Net annual benefit = annual labor savings minus ongoing annual cost

Payback period in months = implementation cost divided by (net annual benefit divided by 12)

First-year total cost = implementation cost plus ongoing annual cost

First-year ROI = (annual labor savings minus first-year total cost) divided by first-year total cost

Multiply the ROI result by 100 to turn it into a percentage. If net annual benefit is zero or negative, there is no payback period under those assumptions. Do not force the formula to produce one.

These formulas treat saved labor as financial value. That is fair only if the freed time is used for higher-value work, avoids a hire, reduces overtime, or creates another measurable benefit. Two quiet hours on Friday may improve someone's week, but they are not automatically cash in the bank.

## A worked example

Consider an illustrative pest-control company. Its office manager spends about ten hours a week on scheduling changes, appointment reminders, and overdue invoice follow-up. The proposed automation handles routine messages, but the manager still reviews exceptions. The company estimates eight hours saved per week.

The manager earns 22 dollars an hour. Payroll and employment overhead bring the loaded cost to 28 dollars an hour.

Annual labor savings = 8 x 52 x 28 = 11,648 dollars.

The company receives a 6,000 dollar implementation quote. Software costs 120 dollars a month, and it budgets another 80 dollars a month for maintenance and small changes.

Ongoing monthly cost = 120 plus 80 = 200 dollars.

Ongoing annual cost = 200 x 12 = 2,400 dollars.

Net annual benefit = 11,648 minus 2,400 = 9,248 dollars.

Monthly net benefit = 9,248 divided by 12 = 770.67 dollars.

Payback period = 6,000 divided by 770.67 = about 7.8 months.

For first-year ROI, include both the build and the running cost:

First-year total cost = 6,000 plus 2,400 = 8,400 dollars.

First-year net return = 11,648 minus 8,400 = 3,248 dollars.

First-year ROI = 3,248 divided by 8,400 = about 38.7 percent.

The arithmetic checks out. The assumptions still need scrutiny. Eight hours saved per week is the number most likely to make or break this case.

## Stress-test the optimistic version

Run the calculation again with a haircut. A simple stress test counts only 75 percent of the expected labor savings.

Conservative annual savings = 11,648 x 0.75 = 8,736 dollars.

Conservative net annual benefit = 8,736 minus 2,400 = 6,336 dollars.

Conservative monthly net benefit = 6,336 divided by 12 = 528 dollars.

Conservative payback = 6,000 divided by 528 = about 11.4 months.

The original case pays back in about 7.8 months. The conservative one takes about 11.4 months. Both land inside a year, but the cushion is much smaller than the first result suggests.

If a project works only when every assumption is generous, it is not a strong project. You can lower the price, narrow the scope, run a pilot, or leave it alone.

## Mistakes that make ROI look better than it is

The easiest mistake is claiming all the current work disappears. Automation usually leaves exceptions, supervision, and cleanup. Count that remaining work.

Watch for double counting too. If faster follow-up is already included as recovered revenue, do not also count every saved admin hour as new sales unless you can show both effects separately.

Other common misses include leaving maintenance out, using a wage instead of a loaded labor cost, and treating freed time as cash without deciding how the business will use it. Implementation quotes can also omit data migration, integration work, employee training, or post-launch fixes. Ask what the quote excludes.

The math is not the hard part. The hard part is refusing to flatter the project.
