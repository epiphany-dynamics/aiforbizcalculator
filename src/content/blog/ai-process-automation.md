**Automate the process with the highest combined score across four factors: how often it runs (frequency), how rule based it is (predictability), how much paid labor it consumes (labor value), and how safely it can fail (failure tolerance). Score each candidate one to five, add the four numbers, and start with the highest total.**

Most owners pick their first automation backwards. They start with a tool they saw in an ad, then hunt for something to point it at. That is how you end up paying a monthly fee to automate a task that runs twice a month. The smarter move is to rank the work first, then buy the tool the winning task actually needs.

## What "automate first" actually means

**Automating first is a ranking problem, not a technology problem. Before you compare tools or prices, you rank your repetitive tasks by expected payback and risk. The best first candidate is frequent, predictable, expensive in labor, and cheap to get wrong, because that combination pays back fast with limited downside.**

The reason order matters is that your first automation sets your appetite for the next one. If the first project pays back in six weeks and nobody gets hurt when it stumbles, you build momentum and budget for the harder projects. If your first swing is a rare, judgment-heavy task where one mistake reaches a customer, you burn cash, spook your team, and quietly shelve the whole idea. Sequencing is the difference between a program and a one-off experiment.

So the job in front of you is not "should we use AI." It is "of the fifteen repetitive things we do, which single one clears the bar first." That is a scoring exercise, and you can do it on one page.

## The four scoring factors

**Each candidate process gets four scores from one to five: frequency (how many times it runs per month), predictability (how often the same rules apply), labor value (loaded hourly cost times hours spent), and failure tolerance (how contained a mistake stays). Higher scores mean stronger, safer automation candidates.**

### Frequency

Frequency is volume per month, and it is the multiplier on everything else. A task that runs 300 times a month turns a tiny per-run saving into real money, while a task that runs four times a month almost never justifies setup effort. Score 5 for daily or many-times-daily work, 3 for weekly, 1 for monthly or rarer.

### Predictability

Predictability is how often the same inputs produce the same correct action. Automation is good at rules and terrible at judgment. Sorting inbound emails by keyword is predictable. Deciding whether to fire a client is not. Score 5 when the same handful of rules covers almost every case, and 1 when nearly every instance needs a human read.

### Labor value

Labor value is the loaded hourly cost of the person doing the task multiplied by the hours it eats each month. Loaded cost means wage plus payroll tax, benefits, and overhead, usually 1.25 to 1.4 times the base wage. A task done by a $22 per hour employee at a loaded $30 for 20 hours a month carries $600 of monthly labor value. Score 5 above roughly $800 a month, 1 below roughly $100.

### Failure tolerance

Failure tolerance is how contained a mistake stays. High tolerance means an error is caught internally, costs a few minutes, and never reaches a customer. Low tolerance means an error hits a client invoice, a legal filing, or a medical record. Score 5 for fully contained, reversible mistakes and 1 for anything that reaches a customer or creates liability.

## The scoring formula

**Add the four factor scores for a Priority Score from 4 to 20. Automate the highest total first. A weighted version multiplies each factor by importance, but the plain sum is accurate enough for a first pass and keeps the math honest. Anything scoring 15 or above is a strong, low-regret first candidate.**

Priority Score = Frequency + Predictability + Labor Value + Failure Tolerance

Use this rubric to assign each one to five score. The numbers below are illustrative anchors, not benchmarks, so adjust the thresholds to your own volume and wages.

| Factor | Score 1 | Score 3 | Score 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Frequency | Monthly or rarer | Weekly | Daily or more |
| Predictability | Mostly judgment | Rules cover about half | Rules cover nearly all |
| Labor value (per month) | Under $100 | Around $400 | Over $800 |
| Failure tolerance | Reaches customer or creates liability | Internal, moderate cost | Fully contained, reversible |

A total of 4 to 9 means leave it alone for now. A total of 10 to 14 means a maybe that depends on tool cost. A total of 15 to 20 means it is a strong first candidate. Score every repetitive process you can name, then sort the list top to bottom. Your first project is line one.

## A worked example

**Consider two illustrative candidates at a small services firm: appointment reminder texts and quarterly contract review. Reminders score high on every factor and total 18. Contract review is valuable but rare, judgment heavy, and high stakes, totaling 8. Reminders win the first slot decisively, and it is not close.**

Here is how the two candidates score under the rubric. All numbers are illustrative.

| Factor | Appointment reminders | Contract review |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency | 5 (about 400 per month) | 1 (about 12 per quarter) |
| Predictability | 5 (fixed template and timing) | 2 (each contract differs) |
| Labor value | 4 (about $650 per month) | 4 (about $700 per month) |
| Failure tolerance | 4 (a missed text is low harm) | 1 (an error creates legal risk) |
| **Priority Score** | **18** | **8** |

Notice that contract review has nearly identical labor value to reminders. If you ranked by cost savings alone, you might pick it. The four-factor score stops you, because its low predictability means constant human correction and its low failure tolerance means a single miss can cost far more than you saved. Reminders pay back fast and fail safely, which is exactly what a first project should do. ## When not to automate the top score first

**Do not automate your highest score if the process is broken, undocumented, or about to change. Automating a bad process just makes the bad output arrive faster. If nobody can write the current steps on one page, fix and document the process first, then automate it. Stability is a prerequisite the score does not capture.**

The scoring model assumes the process is stable and worth keeping. It cannot tell you that your intake form is about to be replaced, that the task exists only because of a workaround, or that two people do the same job three different ways. Before you build, confirm the process will still exist in the same shape a year from now. A high score on a task you are about to retire is a trap, not a green light. When the process is messy, the honest first step is to standardize it, which often recovers time on its own and makes the eventual automation cheaper.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many processes should I score before choosing one?

List every repetitive task you can name in about fifteen minutes, usually ten to twenty for a small business. Score all of them with the four factor rubric and sort by total. You only automate the top one first, but scoring the whole list shows you the pipeline of what comes second and third.

### What if two processes tie on Priority Score?

Break the tie with failure tolerance, then labor value. The safer process to automate first is the one where a mistake stays contained, because your first project is also your team's trust test. If failure tolerance also ties, pick the higher labor value, since that returns cash faster.

### Does a low predictability score mean I can never automate that task?

No, it means it is a poor first candidate. Judgment heavy work can often be partly automated later, where AI drafts and a human approves. Start with clearly rule based work to build skill and budget, then revisit the harder tasks once you have a working process and evidence of payback.

### How do I calculate loaded labor value if I am the one doing the task?

Use what your time is worth to the business, not zero. A reasonable proxy is your target hourly rate or the cost to hire someone to replace that task. Multiply that rate by the hours the task eats each month. Owner time is usually the most expensive labor in the building, which often pushes owner tasks up the ranking.

### Should tool cost be part of the score?

No, keep them separate. The Priority Score ranks which process deserves attention first based on payback potential and risk. Tool and setup cost belongs in the payback step afterward, where you compare the monthly saving against the price. Mixing cost into the score hides which process is genuinely the best opportunity.
